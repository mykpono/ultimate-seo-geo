# E-E-A-T Evaluation Framework
## Updated per Google Quality Rater Guidelines — September 2025 + December 2025 Core Update

---

## Overview

E-E-A-T = **E**xperience, **E**xpertise, **A**uthoritativeness, **T**rustworthiness

**Trustworthiness** is the most important factor — assessed based on the other three plus direct trust indicators.

## CRITICAL: December 2025 Core Update

> **E-E-A-T now applies to ALL competitive queries, not just YMYL.**

The December 2025 core update was a "watershed moment" that:
- Extended E-E-A-T evaluation to virtually all competitive queries
- Made author attribution standards tighter across ALL categories (not just health/finance)
- Penalized anonymous or generic authorship even for non-YMYL content
- Significantly improved AI content quality detection

**Industry traffic impact (Dec 2025):**
| Industry | Traffic Drops |
|---|---|
| Affiliate sites | 71% average decline |
| Health/YMYL | 67% average decline |
| E-commerce | 52% average decline |

**Key takeaway:** Even entertainment and lifestyle content now requires demonstrated expertise. Generic content no longer ranks — regardless of topical category.

## YMYL (Your Money or Your Life)

Topics requiring HIGHEST E-E-A-T standards (though E-E-A-T now matters everywhere):
- Health and safety
- Financial advice and transactions
- Legal information
- News and current events
- **Elections and civic trust** (added Sept 2025)
- **Democratic processes** (added Sept 2025)
- Groups of people (potential for harm)

---

## Experience (Weight: 20%)

First-hand knowledge and personal involvement with the topic.

**Why Experience is critical post-Dec 2025**: AI can generate expertise-sounding content but cannot fabricate genuine experience. This is the primary differentiator.

### Signals to Check
- [ ] Author has demonstrable first-hand experience with the topic
- [ ] Content includes original photos, screenshots, or data
- [ ] Case studies or real-world examples with specific, verifiable details
- [ ] Personal process documentation ("I did X, then Y, and found Z")
- [ ] Before/after results or outcome data with real numbers
- [ ] Specific anecdotes that couldn't be fabricated
- [ ] "I" statements and first-person narrative

### Scoring
- **Strong**: Multiple first-hand experience signals, original content, specific examples
- **Moderate**: Some personal experience evident, occasional first-hand signals
- **Weak**: Generic information, no personal touch, third-person only
- **None**: Clearly AI-generated or scraped content with no unique perspective

---

## Expertise (Weight: 25%)

Formal qualifications, training, and demonstrated knowledge depth.

### Signals to Check
- [ ] Author credentials relevant to topic (bio, certifications, degrees)
- [ ] Technical accuracy and depth appropriate for the target audience
- [ ] Claims supported by evidence or authoritative sources
- [ ] Specialized vocabulary used correctly and naturally
- [ ] Content is current with recent developments in the field
- [ ] Byline with author name and credentials visible on every piece

### Scoring
- **Strong**: Verified credentials, deep technical accuracy, cites primary research
- **Moderate**: Demonstrable knowledge, some credentials mentioned
- **Weak**: Surface-level information, no credentials, generic coverage
- **None**: Factual errors, misinformation, obviously inexpert coverage

---

## Authoritativeness (Weight: 25%)

Recognition by others as a go-to source.

### Signals to Check
- [ ] Site recognized as authority in its specific niche
- [ ] Author cited/mentioned by other authoritative sources
- [ ] Content has earned backlinks from relevant authoritative sites
- [ ] Industry awards, certifications, or accreditations visible
- [ ] Consistent publication history in the topic area
- [ ] Featured in recognized media outlets (interviews, quotes, mentions)
- [ ] Professional affiliations and memberships
- [ ] Speaker/conference presence in the field

### Scoring
- **Strong**: Widely recognized authority, cited by others, industry recognition
- **Moderate**: Growing recognition, some external validation
- **Weak**: No external recognition, little presence outside own site
- **None**: Negative reputation, known for misinformation

---

## Trustworthiness (Weight: 30%)

Most important factor — overall reliability, transparency, and honesty.

### Signals to Check
- [ ] Clear contact information (phone, email, or contact form)
- [ ] Physical address visible (especially for YMYL/local)
- [ ] Privacy policy and terms of service present and current
- [ ] HTTPS with valid SSL certificate and no mixed content
- [ ] Transparent about who creates content and editorial standards
- [ ] Customer reviews, testimonials, or social proof
- [ ] Corrections and update history visible when content changes
- [ ] No deceptive practices (hidden ads, clickbait, misleading claims)
- [ ] Clear distinction between paid content and editorial
- [ ] Return/refund policy (for e-commerce)

### Scoring
- **Strong**: Full transparency, verified business, positive external reputation
- **Moderate**: Good trust signals, minor gaps in transparency
- **Weak**: Missing key trust signals, limited transparency
- **None**: Deceptive practices, scam indicators, negative reviews dominant

## September 2025 QRG Updates

### AI Content Assessment (NEW in Sept 2025)
Google raters now formally evaluate whether content appears AI-generated:
- AI content is **acceptable** if it demonstrates genuine E-E-A-T
- Low-quality AI content (generic, no unique value) is penalized
- The presence of AI-generated content is NOT inherently penalizing
- What matters: does the content provide unique value regardless of creation method?

### Markers of Low-Quality AI Content (Penalized)
- Generic phrasing without specificity
- Lack of original insight or unique perspective
- No first-hand experience signals
- Factual inaccuracies or outdated information
- Repetitive structure across multiple pages
- No author attribution or expertise signals
- Sentences that "feel" like AI: "It's worth noting that...", "In today's world...", "As an AI language model..."

### New Spam Categories (Penalized)
- **Expired domain abuse**: Buying expired domains for their backlinks
- **Site reputation abuse**: Using reputable site to host low-quality content
- **Scaled content abuse**: Mass-producing content without unique value

### Elections and Civic Trust (NEW — Sept 2025)
Added as YMYL category — content about elections, voting, democratic processes requires highest trust standards.

## Overall Scoring Guide

| Score | Description |
|---|---|
| 90-100 | Exceptional E-E-A-T — authority site, recognized expert, full transparency |
| 70-89 | Strong E-E-A-T — demonstrated expertise, good trust signals, some recognition |
| 50-69 | Moderate E-E-A-T — some signals present, meaningful room for improvement |
| 30-49 | Weak E-E-A-T — minimal signals, significant gaps to address |
| 0-29 | Very low E-E-A-T — no visible signals, potential trust issues or penalties |

---

## Improvement Recommendations by Score

### 0-29 (Critical — Fix Immediately)
1. Add contact information and about page with real identity
2. Establish author identity with name and credentials
3. Implement HTTPS
4. Remove any deceptive elements

### 30-49 (Major Gaps)
1. Add author bios with credentials on every article
2. Include first-hand experience signals (case studies, examples)
3. Seek external citations/mentions from relevant sources
4. Add customer testimonials or social proof

### 50-69 (Meaningful Improvement)
1. Deepen content with original research and proprietary data
2. Build topical authority through content clusters
3. Pursue industry recognition (speaking, publications)
4. Document processes and methodologies explicitly

### 70-89 (Optimization)
1. Maintain freshness with regular content updates
2. Expand author presence across platforms (LinkedIn, industry publications)
3. Pursue speaking and writing opportunities in your niche
4. Add video/multimedia demonstrating hands-on expertise

### 90-100 (Maintenance)
1. Continue publishing high-quality, original content
2. Monitor and respond to reputation issues promptly
3. Keep credentials and certifications current
4. Maintain active presence in your professional community

## Quick E-E-A-T Audit Checklist

### Author Page
- [ ] Author page exists at `/about/` or `/author/[name]/`
- [ ] Photo of the author (real, professional)
- [ ] Job title and relevant credentials
- [ ] Years of experience in the field
- [ ] Notable past employers or clients
- [ ] Published works or notable content
- [ ] External links: LinkedIn, professional website, publications
- [ ] Schema markup: Person schema with sameAs array

### Article Level
- [ ] Author byline visible above content
- [ ] Publication date visible
- [ ] Last-updated date visible (if content has been refreshed)
- [ ] Author bio blurb at bottom of article
- [ ] First-hand experience signals in body text
- [ ] External citations to primary sources (at least 3-5)
- [ ] Any original data, research, or unique insights

### Site Level
- [ ] Contact page with real contact method
- [ ] Privacy policy
- [ ] About page with organization description
- [ ] HTTPS enforced
- [ ] Editorial standards page (for publishers)

## E-E-A-T Scoring Framework

| Factor | Weight | Primary Signal | Fix If Absent |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Experience** | 20% | First-hand examples, original photos, real case studies | Add "I did X, found Y" sections; original screenshots |
| **Expertise** | 25% | Author credentials, technical accuracy, byline on every post | Add author bio with credentials and external links to their work |
| **Authoritativeness** | 25% | External citations, industry recognition, authoritative backlinks | Build mentions in niche publications; get quoted by journalists |
| **Trustworthiness** | 30% | Contact info, privacy policy, HTTPS, transparent corrections | Add contact page, privacy policy, clear editorial policy |

**Scoring formula**: Apply each factor weight to its 0–100 score, then sum.
Example: A page scoring 80/25/50/70 = (80×0.20) + (25×0.25) + (50×0.25) + (70×0.30) = 56/100.

## Content Quality Minimums

| Page Type | Word Floor | Keyword Placement |
|---|---|---|
| Blog post | 1,500+ words | Title, H1, first 100 words |
| Service page | 800+ words | Title, H1, first 100 words |
| Homepage | 500+ words | Primary value proposition above fold |
| Product page | 300+ words | Product name in title + H1 + first paragraph |

**Note**: Google explicitly states there is no minimum or maximum word count. These are floors — cover the topic comprehensively. Never pad content to hit a target.

## Readability Scoring

Flesch-Kincaid Grade Level is useful as a content quality diagnostic — not an SEO metric.

| Audience | Target FK Grade | Guideline |
|---|---|---|
| General consumer | Grade 7–9 | Short sentences, plain language, no jargon |
| SMB / professional | Grade 9–12 | Professional tone, some industry vocabulary |
| Technical / developer | Grade 12+ | Accurate terminology expected; don't simplify |

**How to check**: Paste into hemingwayapp.com or use Flesch-Kincaid in MS Word / Google Docs → Tools → Word count.

**Important**: Google does NOT use readability scores as a ranking factor (confirmed by John Mueller). Optimize for your audience's comprehension level, not to hit a grade-level number.

## 2025 Spam Categories (QRG September 2025)

Three new scalable spam categories added to the Quality Rater Guidelines:

| Category | Description | Risk |
|---|---|---|
| **Expired domain abuse** | Buying expired domains for backlink equity, publishing unrelated content | Manual action risk |
| **Site reputation abuse** | Using a reputable site's subdomain/subdirectory to host low-quality third-party content | Manual action risk |
| **Scaled content abuse** | Mass-producing content (AI or human) without genuine value to manipulate rankings | Algorithmic demotion |
